# Large question bank for open questions

## Unit 1 — Financial system

### 1. Explain what haves and havenots are.

**Answer:** Haves have capital surpluses and can lend or invest. Havenots need capital and must borrow or issue securities. The financial system connects both sides through direct, semi-direct or indirect finance.

### 2. How do you define net wealth?

Net wealth = assets - liabilities. Example: a house worth 100 and a mortgage of 80 gives net wealth of 20.

### 3. Compare real assets and financial assets.

Real assets have physical value and utility, such as real estate. Financial assets are claims to future cash flows, such as stocks and bonds.

### 4. What is leverage and why is it dangerous?

Leverage means working with borrowed money. It increases possible ROE if the return on assets is higher than the cost of debt, but it also magnifies losses and can cause solvency problems.

### 5. Direct versus indirect finance?

Direct finance is financing directly between saver and borrower. Indirect finance runs through a financial intermediary, for example bank deposits that are transformed into loans.

## Unit 2/3 — Interest, bonds and money markets

### 6. Explain the Fisher relation.

The nominal interest rate compensates for a real reward and expected inflation. Exact: $(1+r_n)=(1+r_r)(1+\pi^e)$. Approximate: $r_n\approx r_r+\pi^e$.

### 7. Why does a bond price fall when interest rates rise?

Because future coupons and principal are discounted more heavily. Their present value falls, so the bond price falls.

### 8. Difference between spot rate and yield to maturity?

A spot rate belongs to one specific maturity and is used to discount a specific cash flow. YTM is one average discount rate that equates the full bond price with all cash flows.

### 9. What is a T-bill?

A short-term government debt instrument, often sold below face value and repaid at 100. The return comes from the price difference.

## Unit 4 — Equity markets

### 10. What is the difference between primary and secondary market?

In the primary market, the company issues new securities and receives capital. In the secondary market, investors trade existing securities among themselves.

### 11. Which rights does a shareholder have?

Voting rights, possible dividends, a residual claim and the possibility to sell the share on the secondary market.

### 12. Explain the dividend discount model.

A stock is the present value of expected future dividends. With constant growth: $V=D_1/(r-g)$.

## Unit 5 — Risk & return

### 13. Why is diversification useful?

Because assets do not move perfectly together. By combining assets with lower correlation, portfolio volatility can decrease.

### 14. Difference between arithmetic average and growth rate?

The arithmetic average is the ordinary average of annual returns. The growth rate is the compound return that converts the starting value into the ending value. With volatility, the growth rate is usually lower.

### 15. What does correlation mean in portfolio theory?

Correlation measures how returns move together. A lower or negative correlation reduces portfolio risk.

## Unit 6/7 — Derivatives

### 16. What is a derivative?

A contract whose value depends on an underlying asset or variable such as stocks, currencies, interest rates or commodities.

### 17. Forward versus future?

A forward is OTC and customized; a future is standardized, exchange-traded, with margin and marking-to-market.

### 18. What is hedging?

Hedging is reducing an existing exposure with a financial position. For example, an importer buys currency futures to reduce exchange-rate risk.

### 19. Call versus put?

A call gives the right to buy at the strike price. A put gives the right to sell at the strike price.

## Unit 9/10 — Intermediation and banking

### 20. Why do financial intermediaries exist?

They transform maturity, liquidity, risk and size. They make it possible for small, liquid savings to be transformed into larger and longer-term loans.

### 21. Commercial banking versus investment banking?

Commercial banking focuses on deposits, loans and payments. Investment banking focuses on capital markets, underwriting, M&A, trading and advisory services.

### 22. Banking book versus trading book?

The banking book contains positions such as loans that are intended to be held. The trading book contains positions for trading and market making.

## Unit 11 — Crisis 2008

### 23. Explain the 2008 crisis as a chain.

Cheap mortgages and rising house prices led to risky lending. Mortgages were securitized and sold worldwide. When house prices fell, defaults increased. Losses were unclear, confidence disappeared, interbank lending dried up and governments had to intervene.

### 24. Why was Lehman important?

Lehman had large real-estate exposure and went bankrupt. This caused panic because market participants no longer knew which institutions were still safe.

### 25. What is moral hazard in bail-outs?

If institutions expect to be rescued, they may take more risk. Bail-outs may be necessary to avoid a systemic crisis, but they create incentives for risky behaviour.

## Unit 12 — Regulation and supervision

### 26. Regulation versus supervision?

Regulation is the set of rules. Supervision is the monitoring of compliance and risks in practice.

### 27. What is systemic risk?

The risk that problems at one institution or market spread to the entire financial system.

### 28. What is SSM?

Single Supervisory Mechanism: a European supervisory system with the ECB and national authorities. Large banks are directly supervised by the ECB.

### 29. Why is too interconnected to fail more important than only too big to fail?

An institution does not only need to be large; if it is highly connected, its failure can cause a domino effect.
