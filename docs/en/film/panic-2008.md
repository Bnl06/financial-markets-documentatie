# Film — Panic: The Untold Story of the 2008 Financial Crisis

!!! abstract "What you must remember"

    The film shows how a mortgage problem grew into a systemic crisis. The key is not every detail of every date, but the logic: confidence disappears, liquidity dries up and governments must choose between moral hazard and saving the system.

## 1. Beginning: mortgage market

The crisis started in the US housing market. Mortgage sellers sold mortgages to households, often with too little checking of repayment capacity. The assumption was that house prices would continue to rise anyway. This made lending appear safe.

## 2. Securitization

Mortgages were sold on to banks, pooled and transformed into mortgage-backed securities. These packages were sold worldwide. As a result, US real-estate risk was spread throughout the international financial system.

!!! warning "Open question"

    If you are asked what went wrong, do not only say "subprime mortgages". Explain that the problem was amplified by securitization, poor information, high leverage and dependence on confidence.

## 3. Housing bubble bursts

When house prices fell, many households could no longer refinance. Adjustable-rate mortgages became more expensive, defaults increased and foreclosures followed. The value of MBS fell because investors no longer knew which packages were safe.

## 4. Bear Stearns

Bear Stearns had a lot of risky mortgage exposure. When investors lost confidence, Bear Stearns suddenly faced funding problems. The government helped through an emergency solution with JP Morgan because bankruptcy could have damaged the system.

## 5. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac

These institutions were the backbone of the US mortgage market. Although the guarantee was implicit, the market believed the government would help. Because of their systemic role, they were eventually placed under government control.

## 6. Lehman Brothers

Lehman Brothers had enormous real-estate exposure. The government did not want to keep giving bail-outs and no rescue was created/found. On 15 September 2008, Lehman went bankrupt. Afterwards, the interbank market dried up because nobody knew who was safe anymore.

## 7. AIG

AIG was an insurance company, but also highly connected to financial markets. If AIG had failed, large investment banks would also have been hit. That is why AIG was rescued: not only because it was big, but because it was **too interconnected to fail**.

## 8. TARP and the political problem

TARP had to stabilize the banking system. Politically, this was very difficult: citizens saw Wall Street receiving support while ordinary people lost houses, jobs and pension wealth. This shows the tension between economic necessity and a sense of fairness.

## 9. Consequences

The crisis spread from Wall Street to Main Street. Firms had trouble getting credit, payroll and working capital came under pressure, unemployment rose and the real economy was hit.

## Possible exam questions

### Question 1: What was the role of securitization in the crisis?

Answer structure: securitization pools mortgages into securities; this can spread risk, but it made the system complex and opaque. When the underlying mortgages turned out to be weak, nobody knew where the losses were. Confidence disappeared.

### Question 2: Why was AIG important?

AIG was connected to many investment banks through financial protection/insurance. A bankruptcy would have transmitted losses to other major players. That is why AIG was too interconnected to fail.

### Question 3: Why was Lehman not rescued?

The government wanted to avoid moral hazard and faced political resistance against new bail-outs. There was no suitable buyer or solution. The bankruptcy showed how vulnerable the system was and caused a shock of confidence.

### Question 4: What does Wall Street to Main Street mean?

A financial crisis first hits banks and markets, but then the real economy. If credit dries up, companies invest less, produce less and may not be able to finance wages. Unemployment then rises.
